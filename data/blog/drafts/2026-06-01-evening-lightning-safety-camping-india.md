## Direct Answer

If you hear thunder while camping, treat it as a lightning risk: **stop outdoor activity, move to a safer shelter, and wait it out**. Follow IMD thunderstorm/lightning do’s and don’ts and use the **30–30 rule**: if lightning-to-thunder is **30 seconds or less**, seek shelter; wait **30 minutes** after the last thunder before going out again. A tent, open field, ridge, or isolated tree is not a safe shelter.

## Why This Matters Now (Early June 2026)

India’s pre-monsoon and monsoon months regularly bring thunderstorms with lightning. For camping, the risk isn’t just “getting wet” — it’s **being outside, exposed, and far from a proper shelter** when a storm builds faster than your group’s decision-making.

CampIn’s trust-first position: the safest camps are not the “most remote” — they’re the ones where **permission, boundaries, and a clear lightning plan** are defined before anyone arrives.

## The CampIn Lightning Rule: Permission + Shelter + Plan

Before you camp (especially in monsoon months), verify these three things:

1) **Permission**: You’re on explicitly permitted private land or a managed campsite (not “we found a spot”).
2) **Shelter**: There is a **substantial enclosed shelter** you can reach quickly if thunder starts.
3) **Plan**: The host/group leader has a simple “stop-and-shelter” protocol and knows who makes the call.

If any of the above is missing, the best decision is usually: **don’t camp there in storm season**.

## What Counts as “Safer Shelter” (And What Doesn’t)

### Safer (preferred) options

- A substantial enclosed building (closed walls + roof)
- A fully enclosed hard-top vehicle (doors closed)

### Not safe / avoid

- **Tents**, tarps, or open-sided shelters
- Isolated trees, poles, and ridgelines (avoid being the tallest point)
- Open fields, beaches, hilltops, and water edges
- Holding metal objects (trekking poles, fishing rods) in open areas

Note: This is safety guidance, not legal advice. Local rules and property permissions still apply.

## A Simple Lightning Protocol for Campers (Group-Friendly)

Use this as a shared rule set for your WhatsApp group before the trip.

### Step 1: Decide your “stop time”

- If thunder is audible, **end outdoor activity early** and move closer to safer shelter.
- Don’t try to “finish the trek / finish the cook” in exposed areas.

### Step 2: Apply the 30–30 rule

- If the time between lightning flash and thunder is **30 seconds or less**, seek shelter immediately.
- After the last thunder, wait **30 minutes** before returning outside.

### Step 3: If you’re caught outside with no shelter

The real goal is to reduce exposure (not “be brave”):

- Move away from high points, isolated trees, metal fences, and water
- Spread out (don’t huddle the whole group in one tight cluster)
- Avoid lying flat on the ground

If anyone is injured, treat it as a medical emergency and seek help immediately.

## Host Education: Lightning-Safe Camping Is a Product

If you host land/farm/estate stays, you’re not just selling “a view”. In storm months, you’re responsible for **designing predictable safety**.

Minimum host standard for monsoon-ready listings:

- A clearly identified **safe shelter** (building/structure) and a “move now” trigger
- A site layout that keeps tents away from exposed ridges and isolated trees
- A brief check-in message: “If thunder starts, stop activities, move to shelter, and wait”
- A basic communications plan (local network availability, caretaker contact)
- A policy: no late-night wandering or cooking in exposed zones during storms

## FAQs

**Q: Is it safe to stay in a tent during lightning?**

A: A tent is better than being completely exposed to rain, but it is **not** a safe lightning shelter. Plan your camp so you can move quickly to a substantial enclosed shelter (building) or a fully enclosed vehicle when thunder starts.

**Q: What’s the quickest rule to remember?**

A: Use the **30–30 rule**: if lightning-to-thunder is 30 seconds or less, seek shelter; wait 30 minutes after the last thunder before going back out.

**Q: Should we camp near waterfalls or rivers in monsoon?**

A: Avoid camping close to water edges or flood-prone zones in storm season. Choose permissioned private properties with clear boundaries, drainage, and a safe shelter.

**Q: Can lightning alerts help?**

A: Alerts can help you act early, but they don’t replace judgment on-site. Use official local forecasts/warnings and have a conservative “move to shelter” rule when thunder starts.
